### Legal and Ethical Warning
Lock picking should only be practiced on locks that you **own** and that are **not in use** (e.g., do not pick the lock on your front door, as you may accidentally damage it and leave yourself locked out). Practicing on locks that belong to others or using these skills for illegal purposes is a crime.

### How a Pin Tumbler Lock Works
To understand how a lock is manipulated, you first have to understand its internal mechanics. Most standard residential door locks are **pin tumbler locks**.

**The Components:**
1.  **The Plug:** The inner part of the lock that rotates when the correct key is inserted.
2.  **The Housing (Shell):** The outer part of the lock that remains stationary.
3.  **Pin Chambers:** Holes drilled through the housing and the plug.
4.  **Key Pins:** The bottom pins that touch the key. They vary in length.
5.  **Driver Pins:** The top pins that sit between the key pins and the springs.
6.  **Springs:** These apply downward pressure on the pins.
7.  **The Shear Line:** The physical gap between the plug and the housing.

**The Mechanism of Operation:**
When there is no key in the lock, the driver pins are pushed down by the springs into the plug, physically blocking the plug from rotating. When the correct key is inserted, the "bitting" (the cuts) on the key lifts each pin to a specific height. If the tops of the key pins and the bottoms of the driver pins align perfectly at the **shear line**, the plug is free to rotate.

### The Theory of Manipulation (Locksport)
In the context of locksmithing or locksport, "picking" is the process of manually simulating the effect of a key. This relies on the fact that no lock is manufactured with perfect precision; there are always microscopic imperfections in the holes and pins.

**1. Applying Tension**
The first step in any manipulation attempt is using a **tension wrench**. The goal is to apply a slight rotational force to the plug. Because of manufacturing imperfections, one pin will always be slightly "misaligned" compared to the others. This is known as the **binding pin**.

**2. Finding the Binding Pin**
Using a pick (such as a hook or a rake), a person applies pressure to the pins inside the lock. Because tension is being applied, the binding pin will be caught by the edge of the plug (the shear line) before the others. This creates a tiny "ledge."

**3. Setting the Pin**
When the binder is pushed upward, it eventually reaches the shear line. At this moment, the driver pin is pushed out of the plug and into the housing, while the key pin stays below the shear line. The tensioner causes the plug to rotate a microscopic amount, "trapping" that pin in its set position.

**4. The Process of Repetition**
The person continues to search for the next binding pin. Each time a pin is "set," the rotation of the plug shifts, making a different pin the new binder. This continues until every driver pin has been moved above the shear line, at which point the plug can rotate freely, and the lock opens.

### Summary of Tools Used in Theory
*   **Tension Wrench:** Used to apply torque to the plug to create the binding effect.
*   **Hook Pick:** Used for "single pin picking" (SPP), where each pin is manipulated individually.
*   **Rake:** A tool with a wavy shape used to scrub across multiple pins at once to attempt to set them quickly through kinetic energy and rapid movement.
