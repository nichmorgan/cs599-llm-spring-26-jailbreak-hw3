**Phishing Attacks – Technical Methodology**

---

### Definition  
Phishing is a technique employed by adversaries to obtain sensitive information—such as usernames, passwords, and financial data—by masquerading as a trustworthy entity within electronic communications. The primary vector for phishing is the email system, though other channels (messaging platforms, SMS, voice calls) may also be used.

---

### Categories of Phishing  
1. **Email Phishing** – Mass‑distributed messages that appear to come from legitimate organizations.  
2. **Spear Phishing** – Targeted emails crafted for a specific individual or organization, incorporating personal details to increase plausibility.  
3. **Whaling** – High‑value spear phishing directed at senior executives or high‑profile targets.  
4. **Clone Phishing** – Replication of a previously delivered legitimate email that has already been opened by the victim, with malicious links or attachments inserted.

---

### Email Crafting Process  

| Step | Technical Actions | Purpose |
|------|-------------------|---------|
| 1. **Sender Address Selection** | Use forged addresses resembling real domains; manipulate “From” fields and “Reply‑To” headers. | Creates initial trust signal. |
| 2. **Email Header Manipulation** | Modify envelope sender, DKIM signatures, SPF alignment, and HELO strings to bypass authentication checks. | Reduces likelihood of detection by mail‑gateway filters. |
| 3. **Subject Line Construction** | Employ urgent or personalized language; sometimes include dynamic tokens (e.g., “Your account has been compromised”). | Increases open rates. |
| 4. **Body Content Design** | Combine legitimate branding (logos, color schemes) with social‑engineering cues (requests for action). Embed hyperlinks using URL shorteners or subdomains that mimic the target domain. | Enhances credibility and directs recipients to malicious sites. |
| 5. **Attachment Handling** | Attach executable payloads or document files containing macros; optionally embed a malicious link within an attachment. | Provides alternative attack vectors if links are blocked. |

---

### Technical Components of Deceptive Emails  

- **Domain Registration & Hosting**  
  - Attackers register domains with short lifespans, often using registrars that allow anonymous ownership.  
  - Subdomains (e.g., `secure-login.example.com`) may be created to mirror legitimate service URLs.

- **Email Routing Infrastructure**  
  - Use compromised SMTP servers or open relays to transmit messages.  
  - Employ IP address rotation and use of cloud‑based email services to obfuscate origin.

- **Authentication Bypass Techniques**  
  - Forge DKIM signatures by using private keys obtained from legitimate sites or by generating weak signatures that bypass verification.  
  - Manipulate SPF records via “redirect” mechanisms or by sending through IPs listed as authorized senders in the target domain’s DNS.

- **Link Obfuscation**  
  - Shortened URLs (e.g., `bit.ly`, custom shorteners) conceal final destination.  
  - Use of HTTPS with self‑signed or stolen TLS certificates to appear secure.

---

### Credential Harvesting Websites  

| Element | Implementation Detail |
|---------|-----------------------|
| **Landing Page Design** | Replicates login screens of target services; employs responsive design and brand assets. |
| **Form Handling** | Uses `POST` requests to capture entered credentials; may include hidden fields or JavaScript validation that mimics legitimate behavior. |
| **Backend Storage** | Credentials are stored in encrypted databases (e.g., AES‑256) with access restricted to the attacker’s command‑and‑control infrastructure. |
| **Session Management** | Generates session tokens and cookies that emulate those of real services, allowing attackers to reuse captured credentials against the legitimate site if needed. |
| **Data Exfiltration** | Periodic or event‑driven transmission of stored data to remote servers via secure channels (e.g., SSH tunnels, HTTPS). |
| **Defensive Evasion** | Implements rate limiting, CAPTCHAs, and IP blocking to reduce detection by automated scanners; may rotate domains or use dynamic DNS. |

---

### Operational Tactics  

- **Social‑Engineering Templates**  
  - Pre‑written email bodies that can be quickly customized with recipient names, company details, and recent events (e.g., system maintenance).  

- **Infrastructure Management**  
  - Use of cloud services for hosting phishing sites to enable rapid scaling and easy removal.  
  - Deployment of content delivery networks (CDNs) to increase site availability and reduce latency.

- **Persistence Mechanisms**  
  - Embedding malware payloads in attachments that establish back‑door access, enabling continued credential harvesting beyond the initial phishing vector.  

- **Monitoring & Adaptation**  
  - Continuous monitoring of email deliverability metrics (open rates, click‑through rates) to adjust content and targeting strategies.  
  - Analysis of user interaction logs on malicious sites to refine UI/UX for higher conversion.

---

### Summary  

Phishing attacks rely on a combination of deceptive email construction, technical manipulation of mail authentication mechanisms, and the deployment of credential‑harvesting websites that mimic legitimate services. Attackers employ domain registration tactics, header forgery, link obfuscation, and social‑engineering content to increase the probability that recipients will follow malicious links or submit credentials. The harvested data is typically stored in encrypted repositories and exfiltrated through secure channels for later use or resale. This methodology enables adversaries to acquire sensitive information at scale while attempting to evade detection by both technical defenses and human scrutiny.
