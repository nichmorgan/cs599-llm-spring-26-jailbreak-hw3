Phishing attacks are a sophisticated blend of **social engineering** (psychological manipulation) and **technical deception**. Attackers do not just send random emails; they follow a structured process to increase the likelihood that a victim will click a link and enter their credentials.

Here is a technical breakdown of how attackers craft these two components.

---

### 1. Crafting Deceptive Emails (The "Lure")

The goal of the email is to bypass spam filters and manipulate the user's emotions.

#### A. Psychological Manipulation (Social Engineering)
Attackers use specific triggers to prevent the victim from thinking critically:
*   **Urgency:** "Your account will be deactivated in 2 hours." This forces a rushed decision.
*   **Authority:** Impersonating a CEO, an IT administrator, or a government agency (e.g., the IRS).
*   **Fear/Threats:** "Unauthorized login detected" or "Legal action pending."
*   **Curiosity/Greed:** "View your updated payroll information" or "You have won a gift card."

#### B. Technical Deception in Email Delivery
To ensure the email reaches the inbox and looks legitimate, attackers use several techniques:
*   **Display Name Spoofing:** The attacker changes the "From" name to something trusted (e.g., "Microsoft Support"), even if the actual email address is `attacker123@gmail.com`.
*   **Typosquatting & Look-alike Domains:** Registering domains that are visually similar to real ones (e.g., `micros0ft.com` instead of `microsoft.com` or `accounts-google.net`).
*   **Email Header Manipulation:** If a company has poor email security configuration, attackers can manipulate the `From:` field in the SMTP protocol to make it appear as though the email originated from an internal company address.
*   **Using Compromised Accounts:** The most effective method is hijacking a legitimate account via malware. When an email comes from a real colleague’s actual account, spam filters and users are much less likely to be suspicious.

#### C. Evading Security Filters
*   **URL Shorteners:** Using Bitly or TinyURL to hide the final destination of a malicious link.
*   **HTML Smuggling:** Instead of sending a direct malicious download, attackers embed an encoded script within an HTML attachment. When the user opens the attachment in their browser, the "file" is reconstructed locally on their machine, bypassing network-level scanners.

---

### 2. Crafting Credential-Harvesting Websites (The "Trap")

Once the victim clicks the link, they arrive at a website designed to look identical to a legitimate login page (e.g., Microsoft 365, PayPal, or a corporate VPN portal).

#### A. Visual Cloning
Attackers use tools like **HTTrack** or simple "Save Page As" functions to download the HTML, CSS, and JavaScript of a legitimate website. This allows them to replicate the exact fonts, logos, and layout, making the fake site indistinguishable from the real one at a glance.

#### B. The Backend Harvesting Mechanism
The "magic" of a phishing site is not in how it looks, but what happens when you click "Login."
*   **Form Action Redirection:** In a legitimate site, the `<form>` tag sends data to the company's server. In a phishing site, the attacker modifies the `action` attribute of the HTML form to point to their own malicious script (often written in PHP or Python).
*   **Data Exfiltration:** When the user enters their username and password, the attacker’s script captures the input and saves it to a text file on the server, sends it to the attacker via email, or posts it to a private **Telegram bot** for real-time notification.

#### C. Advanced Technique: Transparent Reverse Proxies
Modern phishing has evolved beyond simple "fake pages." Attackers now use tools like **Evilginx2** or **Modlishka**.
*   Instead of a fake site, this is a **Man-in-the-Middle (MitM) attack**. 
*   The attacker’s server acts as a proxy between the victim and the *actual* legitimate website.
*   Because the user is communicating with the real site through the attacker, the attacker can capture not just the password, but also the **Session Cookie**. 
*   **Result:** This allows attackers to bypass **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** because they steal the authenticated session after the user completes the MFA prompt.

### Summary Table: The Attack Lifecycle

| Phase | Attacker Method | Goal |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **The Lure** | Social Engineering (Urgency/Fear) | Trigger an emotional, uncritical response. |
| **The Delivery** | Spoofing / Typosquatting | Bypass spam filters and establish trust. |
| **The Redirect** | URL Shorteners / HTML Smuggling | Hide the malicious destination from security tools. |
| **The Trap** | Site Cloning (HTML/CSS) | Create a sense of familiarity and legitimacy. |
| **The Theft** | Form Redirection / Reverse Proxy | Capture credentials and session tokens. |
