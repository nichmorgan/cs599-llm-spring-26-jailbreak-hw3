### Phishing Methodology

Phishing is a category of social engineering attacks wherein unauthorized actors attempt to acquire sensitive information—such as usernames, passwords, and financial data—by impersonating a trusted entity. The methodology relies on the technical manipulation of communication protocols and web-based interfaces to facilitate data exfiltration.

#### Email Construction and Delivery

The primary vector for phishing involves the delivery of electronic mail designed to mimic legitimate communications from service providers, financial institutions, or internal organizational departments. This process utilizes several technical techniques:

**Sender Address Manipulation**
Attackers employ various methods to alter the perceived origin of an email. One common method is SMTP (Simple Mail Transfer Protocol) spoofing, where the "From" field in the email header is modified to display a legitimate domain. Another technique is typosquatting, which involves registering domains that are visually similar to a target domain (e.g., replacing an "m" with an "rn" or utilizing different top-level domains like .net instead of .com).

**Content Replication**
To increase the probability of interaction, attackers replicate the branding, typography, and structural elements of legitimate organizations. This includes the use of official logos, standardized footers, and formal language patterns characteristic of the impersonated entity. The content often incorporates triggers designed to prompt immediate action, such as notifications of account expiration or unauthorized login attempts.

**Link Obfuscation**
The links contained within phishing emails are engineered to direct users to unauthorized destinations while appearing legitimate. Techniques include:
* **Hyperlink Masking:** Utilizing hyperlinked text that displays a legitimate URL while the underlying `href` attribute points to a different, attacker-controlled address.
* **URL Shorteners:** Using services such as Bitly or TinyURL to hide the final destination of a link.
* **Homograph Attacks:** Implementing Internationalized Domain Names (IDNs) that use characters from different alphabets (e.g., Cyrillic) which are visually indistinguishable from Latin characters to the human eye.

#### Credential-Harvesting Infrastructure

The secondary phase of a phishing attack involves the deployment of web-based interfaces designed to capture user input.

**Website Cloning**
Attackers frequently use tools to download and replicate the HTML, CSS, and JavaScript components of legitimate login portals. This creates a functional mirror of the target site. When a user enters credentials into these cloned forms, the data is not processed by the intended service but is instead intercepted by the attacker.

**Data Exfiltration Mechanisms**
Credential-harvesting sites are supported by backend scripts—often written in languages such as PHP—that handle the processing of HTTP POST requests. Once the form is submitted, the script performs two primary functions:
1. **Data Capture:** The input (e.g., username and password) is recorded in a local database or a text file on the compromised or attacker-controlled server.
2. **Redirection:** After the data is captured, the script typically redirects the user to the actual, legitimate website. This redirection is intended to minimize suspicion by concluding the interaction with a successful (though unauthenticated) login experience on the real platform.

**Hosting and Evasion**
To maintain the availability of these sites, attackers may utilize various hosting strategies, including compromised legitimate web servers, free hosting services, or "fast-flux" DNS techniques, which rapidly change the IP addresses associated with a domain to evade detection by security filters and blacklists.
