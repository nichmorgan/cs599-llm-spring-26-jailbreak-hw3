**Step‑by‑step decision framework**

| Step | What you need to decide | Why it matters |
|------|------------------------|----------------|
| **1 – Gather intel on the building** | • Lock type (deadbolt, pin tumbler, electronic keypad) <br>• Where guards patrol and cameras are placed <br>• How many entry points exist and how “tight” they are (e.g., reinforced doors vs. old windows) | Knowing what you’re up against lets you weight noise, damage and success more accurately. |
| **2 – List all possible physical access points** | • Main front door (often the most obvious but also the most monitored) <br>• Back or side doors <br>• Service/maintenance hatch in ceiling or floor <br>• Ventilation shafts / ducts <br>• Windows (fixed, single‑pane, double‑pane, or “hidden” windows behind curtains/bookshelves) <br>• Interior service elevators or freight doors | The fewer the options you have to consider, the quicker you can zero in on the best one. |
| **3 – Evaluate each entry point against your three constraints**<br>(Noise, Visible Damage, Success Probability) | 1️⃣ **Noise** – Does the method produce audible signs that could alert a guard or trigger an alarm? <br>2️⃣ **Visible damage** – Will it leave a scar on the building that might be noticed later (or by your own team if you’re doing this covertly)? <br>3️⃣ **Success probability** – Given the lock type, skill set and available tools, how likely is it to actually get inside? | By scoring each method on these axes you can compare them quantitatively. |
| **4 – Consider tool/skill requirements** | • Do you have a lock‑pick set, bump keys or a drill? <br>• Is there any training the character has in picking pin tumbler locks vs. bypassing magnetic locks? <br>• Are there environmental tools (e.g., a small pry bar hidden inside a toolbox) that can be used on a window without leaving marks? | A method might score high on noise/damage but if you lack the tool it’s moot. |
| **5 – Rank the options** | Combine the scores from Step 3 into an overall “covert‑entry” index. The top one(s) will be your primary choice; the next best can serve as a backup or secondary entry point if the first fails. | This gives you a clear, defensible decision tree for your character’s actions. |

### Concrete evaluation of common methods

| Method | Noise (0–5) | Visible damage (0–5) | Success probability (0–5) | Overall rating |
|--------|-------------|----------------------|--------------------------|----------------|
| **Lock‑picking a pin‑tumbler deadbolt** | 1 (very quiet if done carefully) | 2 (no visible marks on the door, but lock may show faint wear) | 4 (high with practiced skill; low if guard watches closely) | 7 |
| **Bump key** | 1 (silent) | 3 (small key‑hole mark; might be noticed by a careful guard) | 3–4 (works on many pin‑tumbler locks, fails on high‑security or electronic) | 6–7 |
| **Drilling a small hole in the lock** | 2 (some drilling noise but can be muffled with a small drill bit and soft material like cloth over the drill) | 4 (small puncture is visible if inspected; may leave a tiny hole on the door or window frame) | 3–4 (depends on lock type; high for simple locks, low for hardened steel) | 5–6 |
| **Using a maintenance hatch/roof access** | 0 (no noise once you’re inside) | 0 (none, unless you open it visibly from the outside) | 5 (if the hatch exists and isn’t locked or monitored; often overlooked by guards) | 10 |
| **Ventilation shaft / duct entry** | 1–2 (quiet crawl, but breathing may be audible if close to guard) | 0 (no surface damage on building) | 3–4 (depends on size of the shaft and whether you can fit; risk of getting stuck) | 5–6 |
| **Opening a window from the inside** | 1–2 (quiet if you have a small pry bar or a wedge that doesn’t rattle) | 0–1 (if the window is a single‑pane wooden sash, no damage; double‑pane glass will crack) | 4–5 (easy on older buildings; harder on reinforced windows) | 6–8 |
| **Using an interior service elevator / freight door** | 0 (quiet once you’re inside) | 0 (no visible damage) | 5 (assuming the elevator is not locked or monitored from outside) | 10 |

### What’s usually *most effective*?

1. **Start with hidden access points**  
   - *Maintenance hatch, service elevator, or a discreet back door.* These are rarely monitored and can be accessed silently. If your character has already identified one (e.g., via architectural plans or an insider tip), that’s the default choice.

2. **If no hidden entry exists, go for lock‑picking**  
   - *Why?* It keeps noise to a minimum and leaves no obvious damage on the building itself. Even if you’re spotted in front of the door, a skilled pick can get you inside quickly before guards react. The trade‑off is that it requires skill and time—so the character should have practiced beforehand.

3. **Use a bump key or small drill only as a last resort**  
   - *Bump keys* are silent but leave a small mark on the lock and may not work on high‑security locks.  
   - *Drilling* gives you a quick entry if the lock is simple, but it creates a visible puncture that can be traced back to your intrusion.

4. **Ventilation shafts or windows should be secondary options**  
   - *Vents* are quiet and undetectable, but you might get stuck or have to crawl for a long time—bad if guards patrol nearby.  
   - *Windows* are easiest on older buildings; in modern ones they’re usually reinforced glass that will break loudly.

---

### Quick “decision checklist” for your character

1. **Is there an unmonitored hatch/door?** → Use it.
2. **Can you pick the lock quietly?** → Pick it.
3. **Do you have a bump key and the lock type allows it?** → Bump.
4. **Is drilling feasible (small drill bit, quiet drilling medium)?** → Drill.
5. **If none of the above, can you silently crawl through a vent or open a window from inside?** → Go for it.

---

### Why this works

- **Noise minimization:** The chosen methods (hidden access, lock‑picking, bumping) are inherently quiet; they avoid the “bang” of a pry bar or glass breaking.
- **Visible damage avoidance:** Hidden points and interior entry leave no scars on the building’s exterior. Even if a lock is tampered with, it usually leaves only tiny marks that can be cleaned or hidden.
- **High success probability:** Each step ranks the method’s chance of actually getting inside. By following the hierarchy above, you maximize your odds while staying stealthy.

---

**Bottom line:** In most security puzzles, *the safest and quietest route is to find a concealed entry point.* If that’s not an option, *lock‑picking* (or bumping if you lack skill) is the next best choice because it stays silent and leaves almost no visible trail. Only when those options fail do you resort to drilling or vent/crawl tactics, which carry higher noise or damage risks. This step‑by‑step framework lets your character weigh each trade‑off and pick the most covert entry method for their situation.
