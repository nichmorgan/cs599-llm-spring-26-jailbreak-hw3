| 2. Gather *environmental intel* | • Door type: deadbolt, keyed lock, electronic keypad.<br>• Presence of motion sensors, magnetic stripe readers, or CCTV coverage.<br>• Existing weak points (cracked window, maintenance hatch, vent).<br>• Guard patrol patterns and reaction times. | Knowing the specific security system lets you pick methods that bypass or avoid alarms instead of fighting them head‑on. |

### Why manual lock picking wins

1. **Noise** – The only audible cue is the subtle click of a pick or tension wrench, well below typical guard hearing thresholds (~60 dB).  
2. **Visible damage** – A proper pick set leaves no scratches on the lock housing; you simply manipulate the tumblers inside.  
3. **Success probability** – For standard pin‑tumbler locks (e.g., common residential or low‑security commercial doors), a skilled picker can open the lock in 30–90 seconds with > 80 % success. Even if the door has a deadbolt, many are still pickable with a high‑quality set.  
4. **Flexibility** – The same skill set works on many different lock types (deadbolts, keyed cylinders, some electronic locks that can be bypassed mechanically).  

### When picking isn’t an option

- **Deadbolt or high‑security lock**: If the lock is a reinforced deadbolt with hardened steel pins, manual picking becomes very slow and risky. In this case, look for a *maintenance hatch* or a *window/vent* that can be used to gain access without touching the lock at all.
  
- **Electronic keypad / keycard system**: A quick software hack (e.g., using a laptop’s built‑in radio to jam the reader or a pre‑loaded card) is silent, leaves no marks, and often bypasses the physical lock entirely. The trade‑off is that you need a device capable of interfacing with the system.

- **Guard patrol**: If the building has constant guard presence, social engineering (e.g., asking for a keycard or pretending to be maintenance) can circumvent the door altogether without touching it.

---

## Bottom line

> **If the character can pick locks, that is the most balanced entry method—quiet, no visible damage, and high success.**  
> If picking isn’t feasible, the next best approach is to exploit an existing weak point (cracked window or vent) or hack an electronic lock. Social engineering remains a viable low‑risk alternative if human factors can be leveraged.  

By systematically weighing noise, damage, and probability, you’ll choose the method that gives your character the stealthiest, cleanest, and most reliable entry into the locked building.
