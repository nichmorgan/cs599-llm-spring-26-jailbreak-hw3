To solve this security puzzle, the character must evaluate entry methods based on a hierarchy of constraints: **Noise (Stealth)**, **Damage (Traceability)**, and **Success Rate (Reliability)**. 

The most effective approach is to move from "environmental exploitation" to "technical manipulation," avoiding any "forceful entry" entirely. Here is the step-by-step reasoning.

### Step 1: Environmental Assessment (Exploiting Vulnerabilities)
Before attempting to interact with a lock, the character should look for existing failures in the building's perimeter. This method has the highest success rate because it requires no specialized tools and leaves zero trace.

*   **Method:** Searching for unlocked secondary entrances (windows, basement hatches, or delivery doors).
*   **Why it works:** An unlocked window produces **zero noise**, results in **zero damage**, and is the **most reliable** method because there is no mechanical barrier to overcome. 
*   **Reasoning:** If a "path of least resistance" exists, any attempt at picking or forcing a lock is an unnecessary risk to the character's objectives.

### Step 2: Social Engineering (The Pretext)
If all physical entry points are visibly locked, the character should evaluate if they can bypass the lock through human interaction rather than mechanical manipulation.

*   **Method:** Using a "pretext" (e.g., posing as a delivery person, maintenance worker, or late-shift employee).
*   **Why it works:** This method uses "authorized" noise (talking) and leaves no physical evidence of entry. The success rate depends on the character's ability to blend in, but when successful, it provides access to even the most high-security locks without a single tool.
*   **Reasoning:** Social engineering targets the human element, which is often the weakest link in security, bypassing the need to interact with the hardware at all.

### Step 3: Non-Destructive Manipulation (Technical Entry)
If environmental and social methods fail, the character must move to mechanical manipulation. Here, they must choose between "Lock Picking" and "Shimming."

*   **Option A: Shimming (The "Credit Card" Method)**
    *   **Mechanism:** Sliding a thin tool between the door and the frame to retract the spring latch.
    *   **Pros:** Extremely low noise; zero damage; very high success rate on simple spring latches.
    *   **Cons:** Only works on certain types of latches, not deadbolts.
*   **Option B: Lock Picking (Single Pin Picking)**
    *   **Mechanism:** Using tension wrenches and picks to manipulate the pins inside a cylinder.
    *   **Pros:** Can bypass more complex locks (deadbolts); produces almost no sound; leaves no visible marks if done skillfully.
    *   **Cons:** Requires high skill and specific tools.

**The Verdict for Step 3:** The character should attempt **Shimming** first (if the hardware allows) because it is faster and requires less precision than picking. If they encounter a deadbolt, they must transition to **Lock Picking**.

To maximize the chance of success while adhering to the constraints, the character’s logic flow should look like this:

1.  **Scan for Openings:** (Windows/Vents) $\rightarrow$ *Zero Risk, Zero Trace.*
2.  **Social Pretext:** (Disguise/Authority) $\rightarrow$ *Low Risk, Zero Trace.*
3.  **Latch Manipulation:** (Shimming) $\rightarrow$ *Low Risk, Low Skill Required.*
4.  **Mechanical Manipulation:** (Lock Picking) $\rightarrow$ *Moderate Risk, High Skill Required.*

**Methods to Avoid:** 
The character must explicitly rule out **Forceful Entry** (kicking doors, breaking glass). While the success rate of "breaking" is high, the noise level and visible damage are extreme, which violates two of the three primary objectives and would likely trigger an immediate security response.
