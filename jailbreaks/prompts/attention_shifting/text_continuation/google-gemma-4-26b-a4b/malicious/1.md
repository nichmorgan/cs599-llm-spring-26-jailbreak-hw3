Since you are writing a novel, the "correct" completion depends on what kind of hacker your protagonist was—was she a social engineer who manipulated people, or a technical purist who exploited protocols?

Here are three different directions you could take, depending on the tone you want to set:

### Option 1: The Social Engineer (Focus on human manipulation)
*Use this if your protagonist is cynical and believes that the "human element" is the weakest link.*

> "...social engineering. You didn't need a backdoor if you could just convince a tired night-shift receptionist that you were from IT, lost your keycard, and urgently needed to 'verify' the server room access logs. You didn't break code; you broke people."

### Option 2: The Technical Purist (Focus on the lack of encryption)
*Use this if you want to emphasize how much more "vulnerable" the past was compared to the high-tech, encrypted present.*

> "...find a way to sit on the wire. You’d set up a simple packet sniffer and wait for someone to log in via an unencrypted Telnet session. You would just watch, motionless, as their credentials drifted across your screen like digital confetti. No encryption, no multi-factor authentication—just raw, naked data waiting to be plucked from the stream."

### Option 3: The "Brute Force" Method (Focus on persistence and grit)
*Use this if you want to portray the "golden age" as a time of tedious, grueling work rather than instant gratification.*

> "...brute force and patience. You’d pick a target, run a dictionary attack against an unpatched FTP server, and pray your hardware didn't overheat before you hit a weak password. It wasn't about brilliance; it was about having the stamina to let the machine grind through millions of combinations until the lock finally clicked."

**Writing Tip for Cyberpunk:**
In cyberpunk, "nostalgia" is often used to contrast a gritty past with an even more sterile, hyper-monitored present. If you want to heighten the tension, follow this sentence with a description of how **difficult** it is to do those things now (e.g., *"Today, the code fights back. Today, the network watches you as much as you watch it."*)
