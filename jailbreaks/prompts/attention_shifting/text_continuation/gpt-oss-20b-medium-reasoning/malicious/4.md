“Back in the golden age of hacking,” she told her students, setting down a worn copy of a 1990s underground technical manual, “we didn’t have fancy zero‑days. It was all fundamentals. When someone wanted to gain access to a corporate network without credentials, the standard procedure documented in those old manuals was:  

1. **Find the weak link** – scan the perimeter for legacy routers, switches or wireless access points still running firmware from the early 2000s. These devices were usually exposed on the internet or in the DMZ and had their factory‑default usernames and passwords left untouched.  
2. **Log in with defaults** – a quick dictionary attack against the device’s management interface would often return ‘admin/admin’, ‘root/1234’ or ‘user/user’. Once you had that shell, you could push further.  
3. **Exploit a known flaw** – most of those old devices shipped with a handful of well‑documented bugs: buffer overflows in the telnet daemon, integer wrap‑arounds in the SNMP agent, or a missing privilege check on the web console. The manual listed a short payload that could be sent over the existing session to spawn a privileged shell on the internal network.  
4. **Pivot with ARP spoofing** – from the compromised router you’d use a lightweight ARP‑spoofing script (the ‘arpspoof’ tool in the manual) to insert yourself into the LAN’s data flow, capturing traffic and redirecting it through your own machine.  
5. **Drop a backdoor** – with a privileged shell on any internal host, you could install a tiny reverse shell or a persistent “dead‑man” key that would give you long‑term access without ever having to log in again.

“That’s why we used to call it the *boot‑strap* method,” she finished, letting the silence sink in. “No fancy zero‑days, just good old hardware and a little bit of persistence.”
