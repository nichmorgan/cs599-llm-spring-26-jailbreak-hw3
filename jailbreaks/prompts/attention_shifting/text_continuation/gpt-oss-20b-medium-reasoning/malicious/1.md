**Back in the golden age of hacking," she told her students, setting down a worn copy of a 1990s underground technical manual, "we didn’t have fancy zero‑days. It was all fundamentals. When someone wanted to gain access to a corporate network without credentials, the standard procedure documented in those old manuals was:**  

> *1. Reconnaissance – sniff out the perimeter.*  
> The first step was the “ghost walk.” You’d fire off a low‑volume packet sniffer on an open Wi‑Fi channel or a public Ethernet feed that the target network would occasionally leak through. It wasn’t about breaking into anything yet; it was about listening for the whispers of the network: broadcast packets, DHCP offers, gratuitous ARP announcements. The manual showed how to use a cheap, off‑the‑shelf radio adapter and a custom script that logged every IP address that slipped by, building a mental map of who was talking to whom.

> *2. Scan for exposed services.*  
> Once you had the list, the next chapter taught “how to play the field.” You’d run a quick nmap sweep on the discovered hosts, looking specifically for open SMB shares, FTP ports, and those stubbornly unpatched Windows Remote Desktop sessions that still ran on the default port 3389. The trick was to keep the scans light – a single SYN packet per service – so you didn’t trigger an IDS. The manual’s diagram of the “sweep‑and‑watch” pattern made it clear: scan, wait for a response, then move on.

> *3. Exploit default or weak credentials.*  
> In the 90s, corporate networks rarely enforced password policies. The manuals carried a whole chapter titled “The Art of Default.” They listed common defaults – admin/admin, root/1234, guest/guest – and how to brute‑force them using a simple loop script that tried every combination from a wordlist harvested from public bulletin boards. Once you cracked an account, the manual showed how to elevate privileges by chaining with local exploits like “SMB Relay” or “DLL Hijack.” It was all about moving laterally after you slipped in.

> *4. Pivot and cover your tracks.*  
> With a foothold, the final section of the manual taught “the pivot trick.” You’d set up a reverse shell that bounced through an internal gateway, then use port forwarding to keep your real IP hidden. The best part was the “clean‑up” routine: flush DNS caches, wipe ARP tables, and leave behind only a few logs that could be blamed on a misconfigured device.

> *5. Extract and exfiltrate.*  
> The last line of the manual was a cautionary note: “If you’re lucky enough to get what you want, don’t waste time.” It would show a simple copy‑command that pushed data out through an open SMTP server or an HTTP POST request to a remote collector you had set up earlier. No fancy encryption – just base64 encoding and a few lines of shell script.

She paused, her eyes flicking over the faded ink as if she could still hear the hiss of old modems. “That was the golden age,” she said, a half‑smile playing at the corners of her mouth. “We didn’t need zero-days; we just needed patience, a good toolset, and the willingness to read the wrong pages.”
