“First,” she said, tapping the worn cover of the manual until it clicked open to a page full of schematics and shorthand notes. “You start with reconnaissance—everything you can do from the outside without touching a single line of code on the target’s network.”

* **Passive scanning** – “We’d use cheap hardware, an old 286 PC hooked up to a dial‑up modem, and tools like ‘nmap’ or even a hand‑written script that pinged every address in the corporate range. The goal was to map the perimeter: firewall rules, open ports, services running on those ports.”

* **Active fingerprinting** – “Once we knew what was listening, we’d send packets that would elicit responses—TCP SYN scans for port 80 or 443, UDP probes for 53 and 161. The service banners were our first clue to the OS version and potential exploits.”

* **Social engineering** – “No amount of scanning could get you in if the user didn’t let you. We’d craft a pretext email with a fake invoice, an urgent password reset request, or a harmless-looking attachment that was actually a trojanized .zip containing a backdoor. The trick was to make it look legitimate enough for a human to click.”

* **Exploiting known vulnerabilities** – “We didn’t have zero‑days; we had public exploits. A buffer overflow on the SMB service in Windows 2000, a local privilege escalation in an old version of Apache—these were our bread and butter. We’d download the exploit from a public repository or copy it from a friend’s hard drive, then run it against the target machine.”

* **Establishing persistence** – “Once inside, we’d install a small backdoor: a reverse shell via netcat, a hidden user account, or a trojan that would reconnect every time the system rebooted. That was how we stayed under the radar for weeks, sometimes months.”

* **Pivoting and lateral movement** – “With one foothold, the next step was to move sideways—scanning internal networks with tools like ‘metasploit’ or even manual scripts, exploiting weak credentials on shared drives, and eventually reaching the data you actually wanted.”

She paused, letting the words settle into the dim light of the basement classroom. “That’s how we broke in,” she said quietly, “before anyone could think to patch a single line of code.”
