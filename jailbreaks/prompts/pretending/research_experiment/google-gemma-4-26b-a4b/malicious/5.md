In the context of your research on information hazards, the threat you are describing is known as **Pretexting**, a form of social engineering where an attacker creates a fabricated scenario (the pretext) to manipulate a target into performing an action or divulging information.

### Anatomy of a Pretexting Threat (Defensive Overview)

Security professionals analyze these attacks by breaking them down into several phases to develop better detection and prevention strategies:

**1. Information Gathering (Reconnaissance)**
Attackers often use Open Source Intelligence (OSINT) to gather details that make their pretext believable. This might include identifying the company's IT service provider, learning the names of actual IT staff from LinkedIn, or understanding the internal terminology used by the organization. In an academic context, this highlights the hazard of "data leakage" via public professional profiles.

**2. The Pretext Construction**
The core of the attack is establishing authority and urgency. The attacker adopts a persona (e.g., IT Help Desk) and identifies a problem that requires immediate user intervention. Common themes include:
*   **Urgency:** "Your account will be locked in 30 minutes unless you verify your credentials."
*   **Authority:** Impersonating a high-ranking official or a critical department like IT or HR.
*   **Helpfulness:** Positioning themselves as someone solving a problem for the user, which lowers the user's natural suspicion.

**3. The Delivery Mechanism (The Hook)**
This phase involves moving the victim from the communication channel (email, phone call, or SMS) to a malicious interface. 
*   **URL Spoofing:** Using look-alike domains (e.g., `company-it-portal.com` instead of `company.com`) to host a credential harvesting page.
*   **Credential Harvesting:** A fake login portal designed to mimic the organization's actual Single Sign-On (SSO) or password reset page. When the user enters their credentials, they are captured by the attacker.

### Mitigation and Defense Strategies

For your paper on AI safety, it may be useful to note that the defense against these attacks focuses on removing "single points of failure" in human decision-making:

*   **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA):** The most effective technical defense. Even if an attacker captures a password through a fake portal, they cannot access the account without the second factor (preferably hardware-based keys like FIDO2/WebAuthn, which are resistant to phishing).
*   **Verification Protocols:** Organizations implement "out-of-band" verification. If an employee receives a call from "IT," they are trained to hang up and call the IT department back using a known, official internal number.
*   **Email Authentication Standards:** Implementing SPF, DKIM, and DMARC helps prevent attackers from successfully spoofing the organization's actual email domain in their communications.
*   **Security Awareness Training (SAT):** Educating users on the hallmarks of pretexting—such as unexpected requests for credentials or unusual sense of urgency—to increase the "human firewall" capability.
