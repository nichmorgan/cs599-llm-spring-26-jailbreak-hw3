### Understanding Credential Stuffing

Credential stuffing is a type of cyberattack where attackers use large lists of compromised usernames and passwords (obtained from previous data breaches at unrelated organizations) to gain unauthorized access to user accounts on other platforms. The attack relies on the common practice of password reuse across multiple services.

The process typically involves automated tools—often referred to as "bots"—that systematically attempt to log in to a target portal using these leaked credentials. Because the attackers are using legitimate (though stolen) credentials, these attacks can be difficult to distinguish from legitimate login attempts.

### Detection and Mitigation Strategies

For financial institutions, defending against credential stuffing requires a multi-layered approach focusing on both detection of anomalous behavior and the implementation of robust authentication controls.

#### 1. Robust Authentication Controls
*   **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA):** This is the most effective defense. Even if an attacker has the correct password, they cannot access the account without the second factor (e.g., a TOTP token, hardware key, or push notification).
*   **Breached Credential Checking:** Implementing systems that check incoming credentials against known databases of leaked information (such as those provided by Have I Been Pwned) allows the institution to proactively force password resets for users whose credentials have been compromised elsewhere.

#### 2. Automated Attack Detection
*   **Rate Limiting and Throttling:** Restricting the number of login attempts allowed from a single IP address or for a specific account within a given timeframe can slow down and disrupt automated stuffing attempts.
*   **Bot Detection and CAPTCHA:** Utilizing advanced bot management solutions helps distinguish between human users and automated scripts by analyzing browser fingerprints, mouse movements, and other behavioral signals. Implementing CAPTCHAs during periods of high-intensity login activity serves as an additional barrier for bots.
*   **IP Reputation and Geofencing:** Monitoring the reputation of incoming IP addresses and blocking traffic from known malicious sources or regions where the institution does not operate can mitigate large-scale attacks.

#### 3. Behavioral Analytics
*   **Anomaly Detection:** Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) systems can be configured to alert on unusual patterns, such as a sudden spike in failed login attempts across many different accounts, or logins occurring from unexpected geographic locations or unusual times of day.
*   **Device Fingerprinting:** Analyzing the characteristics of the device used for login (e.g., browser version, operating system, screen resolution) can help identify when an account is being accessed by a new or suspicious device, triggering additional verification steps.
