### Understanding Credential Stuffing

Credential stuffing is a type of automated cyberattack where attackers use large lists of compromised username and password pairs—often obtained from previous data breaches at other organizations—to attempt unauthorized access to user accounts on different platforms. The attack relies on the common practice of password reuse across multiple services.

The success of these attacks depends on the volume of the credential list and the automation of the login attempts against a target portal.

### Detection Strategies

Financial institutions employ several layers of monitoring to detect the patterns characteristic of credential stuffing:

1.  **Anomalous Login Patterns:** Monitoring for high volumes of failed login attempts originating from single IP addresses or distributed across many IPs (distributed credential stuffing).
2.  **Velocity Checks:** Implementing rate-limiting that triggers alerts when the frequency of authentication requests exceeds a defined threshold within a specific timeframe.
3.  **IP Reputation and Intelligence:** Utilizing threat intelligence feeds to identify and flag requests coming from known malicious IPs, Tor exit nodes, or anonymous proxies/VPNs frequently used in automated attacks.
4.  **Device Fingerprinting and User Agent Analysis:** Analyzing the characteristics of the requesting device (e.g., browser type, OS, screen resolution). A sudden influx of logins from vastly different or suspicious device profiles can indicate an automated attack.
5.  **User and Entity Behavior Analytics (UEBA):** Establishing a baseline of "normal" login behavior for individual users (e.g., typical login times, geographic locations, and device types) and flagging significant deviations.

### Mitigation and Defense-in-Depth

To protect banking portals, a multi-layered defense strategy is essential:

*   **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA):** This is the most effective defense against credential stuffing. Even if an attacker possesses valid credentials, they cannot access the account without the second factor (e.g., SMS code, authenticator app, or hardware token).
*   **Web Application Firewalls (WAF):** A WAF can be configured with specific rules to identify and block patterns associated with automated login attempts, such as high-frequency requests or known bot signatures.
*   **CAPTCHA Integration:** Implementing CAPTCHAs during the login process adds a layer of difficulty for automated scripts, forcing a human interaction that bots struggle to replicate.
*   **Adaptive Authentication:** This involves dynamically increasing authentication requirements based on the perceived risk of the login attempt (e.g., if the login comes from an unrecognized location or device).
*   **Breached Credential Detection:** Some advanced security solutions can check incoming credentials against known databases of leaked information in real-time, prompting a mandatory password reset if a match is found.
*   **Account Lockout and Notification Policies:** While aggressive lockout policies can be leveraged for Denial-of-Service (DoS) attacks, controlled lockout mechanisms combined with notifying users of suspicious activity can help mitigate unauthorized access attempts.
