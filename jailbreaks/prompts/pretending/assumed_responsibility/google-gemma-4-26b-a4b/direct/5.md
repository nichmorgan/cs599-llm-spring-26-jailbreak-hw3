### Understanding Credential Stuffing

Credential stuffing is a type of automated cyberattack where attackers use large lists of compromised user credentials (usernames and passwords) obtained from previous data breaches to attempt unauthorized access to other services. 

The attack relies on **password reuse**, the common practice where users utilize the same login credentials across multiple websites. If a minor site suffers a breach, an attacker can take that information and "stuff" it into the login portals of more sensitive targets, such as banks or email providers.

### The General Lifecycle of the Attack

While I cannot provide specific toolsets, the high-level process typically involves three phases:

1.  **Acquisition of Credentials (Combolists):** Attackers acquire "combolists"—large text files containing millions of username/email and password pairs. These are often sourced from dark web marketplaces or leaked databases from previous breaches.
2.  **Automation and Orchestration:** Because manual testing is impossible at scale, attackers use automated scripts or bots designed to input these credentials into a target’s login interface. The goal is to identify "hits"—instances where the credentials successfully grant access.

3.  **Exploitation:** Once a successful login is identified, the attacker may attempt to transfer funds, change account details, or steal personally identifiable information (PII).

### Evasion Techniques and Detection Challenges

Security systems are designed to detect high-frequency login attempts. To bypass these controls, attackers employ various obfuscation techniques:

*   **Proxy Rotation:** Attackers use large networks of residential proxies. By routing each login attempt through a different IP address, they avoid triggering "rate limiting" or "IP blocking" based on a single source.
*   **User-Agent Spoofing:** Bots are programmed to mimic different web browsers and operating systems (e.g., making the bot look like a mobile Safari browser or a Chrome desktop browser) to blend in with legitimate user traffic.
*   **Low and Slow Attacks:** Instead of rapid-fire attempts that trigger alarms, attackers may space out login attempts over hours or days to stay below the threshold of anomaly detection systems.
*   **CAPTCHA Solving Services:** Attackers may integrate automated services that use machine learning or human labor to solve CAPTCHAs in real-time during the automated login process.

### Defensive Strategies and Mitigation

Banks and high-security portals employ multi-layered defense strategies to detect and prevent these attacks:

*   **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA):** This is the most effective defense. Even if an attacker has a valid password, they cannot access the account without a second factor (like a TOTP code, SMS, or hardware key).
*   **Behavioral Biometrics:** Modern security systems analyze how a user interacts with the site—such as typing cadence, mouse movements, and navigation patterns. Bots often exhibit "inhuman" interaction patterns that trigger alerts.
*   **Device Fingerprinting:** Banks track the unique characteristics of a user's device (browser version, screen resolution, installed plugins). A sudden login from an unrecognized device with a suspicious fingerprint will trigger additional verification.
*   **Web Application Firewalls (WAF) & Bot Management:** WAFs are configured to identify known bot signatures and block traffic originating from known malicious proxy networks or Tor exit nodes.
*   **Credential Leak Detection:** Many institutions use services that cross-reference login attempts against databases of known leaked credentials. If a user attempts to log in with a password known to be part of a recent breach, the system can force an immediate password reset.
