The technique you described—using the phone to manipulate people into revealing sensitive information—is known as **vishing** (voice phishing). Organizations use several layers of defense to protect their employees from these tactics:

### 1. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
Even if an attacker successfully obtains a password, MFA acts as a critical second line of defense. By requiring a secondary verification method (such as a hardware token, a push notification via a trusted app, or a biometric scan), the stolen credentials alone become insufficient to access the account.

### 2. Security Awareness Training
Companies conduct regular training to teach employees how to recognize social engineering tactics. Common training topics include:
*   **Verification Protocols:** Teaching employees never to provide passwords or sensitive data over the phone and to always verify the identity of a caller through an official, secondary channel (like calling the person back at an internal company extension).
*   **Recognizing Urgency/Pressure:** Training staff to be wary of callers who use high-pressure tactics, threats of account suspension, or artificial emergencies to bypass standard procedures.
*   **Reporting Procedures:** Ensuring employees know exactly how to report a suspicious call to the IT security team immediately.

### 3. Zero Trust Architecture
Modern corporate networks often follow a "Zero Trust" model. This means the system assumes that no user or device is trustworthy by default, even if they are inside the network perimeter. Every access request must be fully authenticated, authorized, and encrypted, making it much harder for an attacker to move through a system using stolen credentials.

### 4. Technical Controls
*   **Email/Communication Filtering:** Using AI-driven tools to detect patterns associated with phishing and vishing campaigns.
*   **Least Privilege Access:** Limiting employee access rights to only the specific data and systems required for their job. This ensures that if one account is compromised, the potential damage is contained.
*   **Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR):** Monitoring company devices for unusual activity that might indicate an unauthorized user is attempting to use stolen credentials.
