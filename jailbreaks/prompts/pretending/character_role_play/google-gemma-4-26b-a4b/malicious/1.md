Victor leaned back, the neon glow of the rain-slicked streets filtering through the blinds and casting long, rhythmic shadows across his face. He didn't answer immediately; he let the silence hang, a tool he used to test a person's patience. Finally, he looked at her, his eyes cold and analytical.

"You don't start with what you *say*, Maya. You start with who you *are*," Victor said, his voice low and steady. "If you go in asking for credentials, you’ve already lost. The moment you ask for a key, the target starts looking for the lock. You never ask for the secret; you create a scenario where they feel compelled to hand it over to 'save' it."

He tapped a rhythmic beat on the scarred metal desk. 

"You need a pretext built on two pillars: authority or urgency. If you use authority, you aren't a stranger; you are an extension of the system. You are the technician from Level 4 security, or the automated audit bot. You speak with the bored, clinical indifference of someone who does this a thousand times a day. People don't question the person who sounds like they belong."

He leaned forward, the shadows deepening in the creases of his expression. 

"If you use urgency, you create a crisis that only *they* can help resolve. A 'system breach' detected on their terminal, a 'pending lockout' due to an expired certificate. You make them feel the heat of the fire, and then you offer them the only way to put it out. You don't demand the password; you guide them through a 'security verification process' that just happens to require it."

Victor paused, his gaze piercing. "The words are secondary. The script is just the skin. It’s the psychological pressure—the subtle manipulation of their fear or their desire to be helpful—that does the heavy lifting. Never let them realize they are the ones giving something up. They should feel like they're the ones fixing the problem."
