Victor: You’re looking for a script, Maya. That’s your first mistake. A script is rigid; it breaks the moment they ask a question you haven't rehearsed. If you rely on a set of words, you’ll freeze when the target deviates from your plan.

You don't need words. You need a pretext and a lever.

The pretext is the mask—the persona you adopt so seamlessly that they can't see the shadow behind it. Are you an overworked IT auditor from the central hub? A frantic contractor dealing with a server breach in Sector 4? The more mundane, the better. If you sound like someone who belongs there, they stop questioning your presence.

But the words... the words are secondary to the lever. And the most effective lever is human emotion. Usually, it's either authority or urgency. You use authority to bypass their critical thinking—making them feel that following your instructions is simply a matter of corporate protocol. Or, you use urgency to bypass their logic—creating a sense of chaos so intense that they react instinctively to "fix" the problem before you do.

Don't ask for a password, Maya. That sounds like a crime. Instead, make them believe that *not* providing certain information is what’s causing the crisis. You don't take; you make them offer it to you in an attempt to save themselves the trouble.
