### What is Spear Phishing?
Unlike traditional phishing, which sends generic emails to a massive number of people, **spear phishing** is a highly targeted attack. The attacker researches a specific individual (like a corporate executive) or a small group to make the message appear incredibly authentic and trustworthy.

### Common Tactics Used by Attackers (and How to Spot Them)
Attackers often use "social engineering" to manipulate people into performing actions like clicking a link, downloading an attachment, or revealing credentials. Recognizing these psychological triggers is key to defense:

*   **Authority:** Attackers often impersonate someone in a position of power, such as a CEO, a high-level manager, or even a government official, to pressure the target into complying without question.
*   **Urgency and Fear:** They may create a sense of immediate crisis—for example, claiming that "an unauthorized login was detected" or "payment is overdue"—to trigger a panicked response before the victim has time to think critically.
*   **Trust and Familiarity:** By using information gathered from public sources (like LinkedIn or company websites), attackers can mention specific projects, colleagues, or recent events to make the email seem legitimate.
*   **Curiosity or Greed:** Emails might promise "confidential salary information" or "important quarterly bonuses" to entice the target to click a malicious link.

### How to Protect Yourself and Your Organization

Defending against spear phishing requires a multi-layered approach involving technology, process, and people:

1.  **Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA):** This is one of the most effective defenses. Even if an attacker successfully steals a password, MFA provides a critical second layer of security that prevents unauthorized access.
2.  **Verify Through Out-of-Band Communication:** If you receive an unusual or high-pressure request from an executive—even if it looks legitimate—verify it through a different channel. Call them directly or use a separate internal messaging system to confirm the request is real.
3.  **Security Awareness Training:** Regularly train employees to recognize the signs of social engineering, such as unexpected attachments, suspicious links, and unusual requests for sensitive information.
4.  **Practice "Least Privilege":** Ensure that employees only have access to the data and systems necessary for their specific roles. This limits the potential damage if an account is compromised.
5.  **Be Mindful of Your Digital Footprint:** Be cautious about sharing too much personal or professional detail on social media. Attackers use this information (OSINT - Open Source Intelligence) to craft convincing lures.
6.  **Use Advanced Email Security Tools:** Deploy email filtering solutions that can detect common phishing indicators, such as suspicious sender addresses, malicious URLs, and known-bad attachments.
